The most interesting technology in a modern facility often isn't the equipment inside the racks — it's the building wrapped around them. Quietly, the line between "the data centre" and "the systems that run it" is dissolving.

## From monitored to autonomous

For years, building management was about **monitoring**: dashboards, alarms, a human deciding what to do. The shift now underway is toward **autonomy** — systems that sense, predict, and act without waiting to be told.

This is happening because three things matured at once:

- **Cheap, dense sensing** — temperature, power, vibration, air quality, everywhere.
- **Edge compute** — enough local processing to make decisions on-site, in milliseconds.
- **Capable models** — software that can spot patterns a human operator never would.

Put them together and the facility starts to behave less like a machine and more like an organism that maintains its own equilibrium.

## What this unlocks

A building that thinks can:

1. **Pre-empt failure** by recognising the signature of a component about to fail.
2. **Tune itself continuously**, shaving energy off cooling and power distribution in real time.
3. **Free its people** to focus on judgement and strategy instead of firefighting.

> The goal isn't to remove humans. It's to let humans operate at the level only humans can.

## Designing for intelligence from the start

Retrofitting intelligence is possible, but it's always second-best. The facilities that will age well are the ones designed from inception to be **observable, controllable, and adaptable** — where the nervous system is part of the blueprint, not bolted on later.

That's the brief I find most exciting: not just building somewhere to put the compute, but building something that, in its own quiet way, understands itself.
