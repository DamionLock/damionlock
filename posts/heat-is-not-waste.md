---
title: Heat is not waste
topic: Sustainability
date: 2026-04-28
---

Walk past any data centre and you'll feel it: the low roar of fans pushing out warm air. For decades we've treated that heat as a problem to be expelled as fast and cheaply as possible. That instinct is starting to look like a missed opportunity.

## A by-product worth keeping

Servers are, in effect, very expensive heaters. Nearly all the electricity they consume ends up as low-grade warmth. The conventional response is to spend *even more* energy throwing that warmth away.

But low-grade heat is genuinely useful:

- **District heating** networks can pipe it into homes and offices.
- **Greenhouses** and aquaculture can grow food with it year-round.
- **Industrial processes** can use it as a pre-heat stage, cutting fuel demand.

The technology to capture and move this heat is mature. What's usually missing is the **intent** to design for it from day one.

## Designing facilities as neighbours

The shift is philosophical as much as technical. A data centre stops being a sealed box on a business park and becomes a piece of local infrastructure — a neighbour that gives something back.

> The most sustainable kilowatt-hour is the one that does two jobs.

That changes where we site facilities, how we negotiate with communities, and which metrics we report. Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) tells you how efficiently you run the box. It says nothing about whether the heat leaving the box did anyone any good.

## A better scorecard

If we're serious about sustainable compute, "energy reused" deserves to sit alongside PUE and water usage on every facility scorecard. Heat isn't the embarrassing exhaust of digital infrastructure. Handled well, it's one of its most valuable outputs.
